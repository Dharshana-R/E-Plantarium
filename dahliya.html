DAHLIYA




Dahlia is a genus of bushy, tuberous, herbaceous perennial plants native to Mexico and Central America. A member of the family of dicotyledonous plants, its garden relatives thus include the sunflower, daisy, chrysanthemum, and zinnia. There are 42 species of dahlia, with hybrids commonly grown as garden plants. Flower forms are variable, with one head per stem; these can be as small as 5 cm (2 in) diameter or up to 30 cm (1 ft) ("dinner plate").
This great variety results from dahlias being octoploids—that is, they have eight sets of homologous chromosomes, whereas most plants have only two. In addition, dahlias also contain many transposons—genetic pieces that move from place to place upon an allele—which contributes to their manifesting such great diversity.

Pests and Diseases:

Among the most common dahlia diseases are those caused by fungi, such as powdery mildew and gray mold.
Dahlia flower pests tend to reflect the sap drinking forms but can also encompass leaf eaters such as a wide array of caterpillars and larvae.

Additional Habitat Information:

Dahlia is found predominantly in Mexico, but some species are found ranging as far south as northern South America. parts of Central America and northern South America. Dahlia is a genus of the uplands and mountains, being found at elevations between 1,500 and 3,700 meters, in what has been described as a "pine-oak woodland" vegetative zone. Most species have limited ranges scattered throughout many mountain ranges in Mexico.


Use:

Dahlia, Dahlia pinnata is the herb which is used to treat epilepsy, skin problems such as rashes, cracks and soothe tired feet.
